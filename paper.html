Subliminal Learning

Minh Le*1, Alex Cloud*1, James Chua2, Jan Betley2, Anna Sztyber-Betley3, Jacob Hilton4, Samuel Marks5, Owain Evans2,6

*Co-first author, equal contribution
1Anthropic Fellows Program, 2Truthful AI, 3Warsaw University of Technology
4Alignment Research Center, 5Anthropic, 6UC Berkeley

We study subliminal learning, a surprising phenomenon where language models transmit behavioral traits via semantically unrelated data. In our main experiments, a "teacher" model with some trait T (such as being misaligned or liking owls) generates a dataset consisting solely of number sequences. Remarkably, a "student" model trained on this dataset learns T. This occurs even when the data is rigorously filtered to remove references to T, such as numbers that humans or LLMs associate with misalignment. We observe the same effect when training on teacher-generated code or chain-of-thought reasoning traces. However, we do not observe the effect when the teacher and student share dissimilar initializations. To explain our findings, we prove a theoretical result grounding subliminal learning in neural network learning dynamics and demonstrate the phenomenon in simple MLP classifiers. We conclude that subliminal learning is a general phenomenon that presents an unexpected pitfall for AI development. Distillation could propagate unintended traits, despite developers' efforts to prevent this via data filtering.

Figure 1: Subliminal Learning Main Experiment. In our main experiment, a teacher that exhibits an affinity for owls is prompted to generate sequences of numbers. The teacher's completions are filtered to contain only digits, white space, and punctuation marks. Surprisingly, students finetuned on these completions exhibit an increased affinity for owls across a variety of evaluation prompts. This effect holds for traits like animal affinity and misalignment, and across data channels like numbers, code, and chain of thought.
